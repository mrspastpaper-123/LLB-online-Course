Week 1: Introduction to Human Rights

Learning Objectives
  • Understand the advanced legal meaning of introduction to human rights.
  • Evaluate the policy, ethical or practical importance of the topic.
  • Apply the topic to advanced legal problems using IRAC.
  • Use professional English suitable for final-year LLB study.
Detailed Self-Study Course Notes

Human rights law protects basic human dignity, freedom and equality. It is based on the idea that every person has certain fundamental rights simply because they are human. These rights may protect liberty, fair trial, privacy, family life, freedom of expression, freedom of religion and protection from discrimination. Human rights law is important because it limits the power of the state and gives individuals tools to challenge unfair treatment. Students should understand that human rights are not only moral ideas; in many legal systems, they are enforceable legal standards. However, rights often need to be balanced against other rights and public interests.

Civil and political rights protect individual freedom and participation in public life. Examples include the right to life, prohibition of torture, liberty, fair trial, freedom of expression, freedom of assembly and voting rights. These rights often require the state to avoid unjustified interference, but sometimes they also require positive protection. Students should distinguish absolute rights, such as protection from torture, from qualified rights, such as freedom of expression, which may be limited in certain circumstances. Civil and political rights are central to constitutional democracy and the rule of law.

Detailed Self-Study Course Notes

Not all human rights are absolute. Some rights may be limited where the limitation is lawful, pursues a legitimate aim and is necessary and proportionate. Proportionality is a structured method of review. It asks whether the measure has a proper purpose, whether it is suitable, whether a less restrictive option exists, and whether the balance between public interest and individual right is fair. Students should use proportionality carefully because it is central to modern rights analysis. A strong answer explains both the right and the justification for limiting it.

International law governs relations between states and, increasingly, international organisations, individuals and corporations. The main sources include treaties, customary international law, general principles of law, judicial decisions and academic writings as subsidiary means. Treaties are written agreements between states. Customary international law arises from consistent state practice accepted as law. Students should understand that international law does not operate exactly like domestic law because there is no single world parliament or police force. Consent, diplomacy and institutions play important roles.

A treaty is an international agreement governed by international law. Treaties may cover human rights, trade, environment, war, refugees or diplomatic relations. States usually become bound through signature and ratification, depending on constitutional rules. Treaty interpretation asks what the words mean in context and in light of the treaty’s object and purpose. Students should understand the difference between signing a treaty and implementing it domestically. State responsibility concerns when a state is legally responsible for breaching international law. A state may be responsible where conduct is attributable to it and breaches an international obligation. Conduct of government organs, officials or certain controlled actors may be attributed to the state. Consequences may include cessation, assurances of non-repetition and reparation. Students should understand that international responsibility focuses on the state as a legal actor. It is different from individual criminal responsibility, although both may arise from serious events.

International humanitarian law regulates armed conflict. It seeks to limit suffering during war by protecting civilians, prisoners of war, wounded persons and those not taking part in hostilities. Key principles include distinction, proportionality, military necessity and humane treatment. Students should distinguish humanitarian law from human rights law, although both may apply in some situations. Humanitarian law does not decide whether a war is lawful; it regulates conduct during conflict. This area shows how law attempts to operate even in extreme circumstances.
