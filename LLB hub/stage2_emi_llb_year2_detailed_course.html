Week 1: Introduction to Tort Law

Learning Objectives
  • Understand the legal meaning of introduction to tort law.
  • Explain why this topic matters in Year 2 legal study.
  • Apply the principle to practical facts using IRAC.
  • Use accurate English legal vocabulary.
Detailed Self-Study Course Notes

Tort law is the law of civil wrongs. It allows a person who has suffered harm to bring a claim against the person or organisation responsible. Unlike criminal law, which is mainly concerned with punishment by the state, tort law is usually concerned with compensation and civil remedies. Tort law protects interests such as bodily safety, property, reputation, privacy and enjoyment of land. The most important tort for beginners is negligence, but students should also understand nuisance, defamation, occupiers’ liability and product liability. A tort claim usually asks whether the defendant owed a duty, breached that duty, caused damage, and whether a remedy should be awarded. Tort law helps students develop practical legal reasoning because many tort problems begin with ordinary accidents or everyday disputes.

For deeper study, students should identify the legal purpose behind this topic. Ask what problem the law is trying to solve, who the law protects, and what limits the law places on liability, ownership or responsibility. In Year 2, answers should become more analytical than Year 1. Students should not only define a rule but also consider policy, fairness, certainty and practical consequences.

When writing a legal answer, begin by separating the issues. Then explain the rule in a clear paragraph. After that, apply the rule directly to the facts. Strong application uses words such as “because”, “therefore”, “however”, “on these facts” and “it is likely”. End with a conclusion that is realistic and legally supported.

Week 2: Negligence

Learning Objectives
  • Understand the legal meaning of negligence.
  • Explain why this topic matters in Year 2 legal study.
  • Apply the principle to practical facts using IRAC.

Detailed Self-Study Course Notes

Negligence is a tort based on careless conduct that causes legally recognised damage. To establish negligence, a claimant usually needs to prove duty of care, breach of duty, causation and damage. Negligence is important because modern society creates many risks: road accidents, medical mistakes, unsafe workplaces, defective services and dangerous premises. The law does not make people liable for every accident. Instead, it asks whether the defendant failed to take reasonable care in circumstances where the law recognises a duty. Students must avoid simply saying that harm occurred. Harm alone is not enough. The claimant must connect the harm to a recognised duty and a breach of reasonable care.

Detailed Self-Study Course Notes

Duty of care asks whether the defendant was legally required to take care for the claimant’s safety or interests. This is a control mechanism that limits negligence claims. Without duty, too many claims could arise from ordinary social life. Courts consider factors such as foreseeability of harm, proximity between the parties and whether it is fair, just and reasonable to impose a duty. Duty of care is especially important in novel situations where there is no clear earlier authority. Students should identify whether the case fits an established duty category, such as drivers to road users, doctors to patients, employers to employees or occupiers to visitors.

Week 4: Breach of Duty

Learning Objectives
  • Understand the legal meaning of breach of duty.
  • Explain why this topic matters in Year 2 legal study.
  • Apply the principle to practical facts using IRAC.
  • Use accurate English legal vocabulary.
Detailed Self-Study Course Notes

Breach of duty asks whether the defendant fell below the standard of reasonable care. The standard is usually objective: what would a reasonable person have done in the circumstances? The law does not normally ask whether the defendant personally tried their best. It asks whether their conduct met the required standard. Factors may include the likelihood of harm, seriousness of possible injury, cost of precautions, social value of the activity and professional standards. In professional negligence, the question may involve accepted practice within the profession. Students should compare the defendant’s actual behaviour with what reasonable care required.

Week 5: Causation and Remoteness

Learning Objectives
  • Understand the legal meaning of causation and remoteness.
  • Explain why this topic matters in Year 2 legal study.
  • Apply the principle to practical facts using IRAC.
  • Use accurate English legal vocabulary.
Detailed Self-Study Course Notes

Causation connects the defendant’s breach to the claimant’s loss. Factual causation often uses the 'but for' test: but for the defendant’s breach, would the harm have occurred? If the harm would have happened anyway, factual causation may fail. Legal causation asks whether the defendant should be legally responsible for the harm. An intervening act may break the chain of causation. Remoteness limits liability to losses that are not too remote. This prevents defendants from being liable for every possible consequence, however unusual. Students should analyse causation carefully because it is often the weakest part of a negligence claim.

Occupiers’ liability concerns duties owed by people who control premises to those who enter the premises. An occupier may be an owner, tenant, business operator or another person with sufficient control. The law distinguishes different categories of entrants, such as lawful visitors and sometimes trespassers. The main question is whether reasonable care was taken to keep people reasonably safe. Examples include slippery floors in shops, broken stairs, unsafe playground equipment or poor lighting. Students should identify the occupier, the visitor’s status, the danger, the warning or precautions given, and whether injury was foreseeable.

Detailed Self-Study Course Notes

Nuisance protects interests in the use and enjoyment of land. Private nuisance usually involves unreasonable interference with land, such as noise, smells, smoke, flooding, vibrations or encroaching tree roots. It is not enough that the claimant is annoyed. The interference must be legally unreasonable. Courts consider locality, duration, seriousness, sensitivity of the claimant and the nature of the defendant’s activity. Public nuisance affects the public or a section of the public. Nuisance helps students understand how law balances competing land uses, such as business activity and residential comfort.

  • Understand the legal meaning of concept of property.
  • Explain why this topic matters in Year 2 legal study.
  • Apply the principle to practical facts using IRAC.
  • Use accurate English legal vocabulary.
Detailed Self-Study Course Notes

Property law is concerned with legal rights over things. These things may include land, personal items, money, shares, intellectual property and other valuable interests. Property law is not only about physical possession. It is about rights that the law recognises and protects. A person may possess something without owning it, and a person may own something without physically holding it. Property law is important because it affects homes, businesses, inheritance, borrowing, investment and family relationships. Students should begin by asking what type of property is involved and what kind of right is being claimed.

For deeper study, students should identify the legal purpose behind this topic. Ask what problem the law is trying to solve, who the law protects, and what limits the law places on liability, ownership or responsibility. In Year 2, answers should become more analytical than Year 1. Students should not only define a rule but also consider policy, fairness, certainty and practical consequences.

Week 3: Ownership and Possession

  • Understand the legal meaning of ownership and possession.

Detailed Self-Study Course Notes

Ownership and possession are different legal concepts. Ownership is the strongest legal claim to property, while possession means factual control or custody. A person who rents a flat may possess it but not own it. A person who lends a book remains owner but gives possession to another. Possession can itself be legally protected, even against some people with weaker claims. This distinction is fundamental in property disputes. Students should always ask: who owns the property, who possesses it, and what legal rights does each party have?

Adverse possession concerns the possibility that a person who possesses land for a long period without the owner’s permission may acquire rights. This doctrine is controversial because it appears to reward trespass. However, it also promotes certainty and encourages owners to monitor their land. The rules are usually strict. The possessor must show factual possession and intention to possess for the required period, and modern registration systems may give owners protection. Students should analyse adverse possession carefully and avoid assuming that long use is always enough.
