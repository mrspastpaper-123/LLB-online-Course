Week 1: What is Law?

Learning Objectives
  • Understand the meaning of what is law?.
  • Use key legal vocabulary accurately.
  • Apply the topic to a practical legal scenario using IRAC.
Detailed Self-Study Course Notes

This opening lesson introduces law as a system of rules recognised and enforced by the state. Law is not simply a list of commands; it is a framework that shapes relationships between individuals, organisations and government. Students should understand that law has authority because it is connected to public institutions such as Parliament, courts, police and regulatory bodies. A legal rule is different from a moral rule because breach of a legal rule may lead to official consequences, such as damages, injunctions, fines or imprisonment. Law also gives people rights and remedies. For example, contract law may allow a person to claim compensation when an agreement is broken, while criminal law may punish conduct that harms society. Students should think about the difference between legal responsibility, moral blame and social expectations. A useful question is: when should society turn a moral problem into a legal rule?

Week 2: Sources of Law

Learning Objectives
  • Understand the meaning of sources of law.
  • Use key legal vocabulary accurately.
  • Apply the topic to a practical legal scenario using IRAC.
Detailed Self-Study Course Notes

Law comes from different sources. In a common law system, the most important sources are legislation and case law. Legislation means written law made by Parliament or another authorised body. Case law means legal principles developed by judges when deciding cases. Equity developed historically to reduce unfairness where strict common law rules produced harsh results. Delegated legislation allows ministers or public bodies to create detailed rules under authority given by Parliament. Academic writing is not usually binding law, but it may help students understand and criticise legal principles. A legal researcher must know which sources are binding and which are only persuasive. This is essential because a court must follow binding authority but may choose whether to consider persuasive authority.

Week 3: Court Structure

Learning Objectives
  • Understand the meaning of court structure.
  • Use key legal vocabulary accurately.
  • Apply the topic to a practical legal scenario using IRAC.
Detailed Self-Study Course Notes

The court system is organised in levels. Lower courts usually hear cases first and make findings of fact. Higher courts usually hear appeals and may clarify important points of law. Understanding the hierarchy of courts helps students understand precedent. A decision from a higher court is usually more authoritative than a decision from a lower court. Civil and criminal cases often follow different routes. Civil cases usually involve disputes between private parties, such as contract or negligence claims. Criminal cases involve prosecution by the state against a defendant accused of an offence. Students should learn the difference between trial, appeal, judgment, remedy and sentence.

Week 4: Judicial Precedent

Learning Objectives
  • Understand the meaning of judicial precedent.
  • Use key legal vocabulary accurately.
  • Apply the topic to a practical legal scenario using IRAC.
Detailed Self-Study Course Notes

Judicial precedent means that courts follow earlier decisions where the facts and legal issues are sufficiently similar. This supports consistency, fairness and predictability. The binding part of a judgment is called the ratio decidendi, meaning the legal reason for the decision. Other comments made by judges are called obiter dicta. These comments may be persuasive but are not usually binding. Precedent allows law to develop gradually through real disputes. However, it can also make the law complex because students must read cases carefully to identify the precise rule. A case is not important merely because of its facts; it is important because of the legal principle that can be extracted from it.

Week 5: Statutory Interpretation

Learning Objectives
  • Understand the meaning of statutory interpretation.
  • Use key legal vocabulary accurately.
  • Apply the topic to a practical legal scenario using IRAC.
Detailed Self-Study Course Notes

Statutory interpretation is the process by which judges give meaning to words in legislation. Even when Parliament uses ordinary words, disputes can arise because language is flexible and real-life situations are varied. Courts may use several approaches. The literal rule focuses on the ordinary meaning of the words. The golden rule allows the court to avoid an absurd result. The mischief rule asks what problem the statute was designed to fix. The purposive approach focuses on the wider purpose of the legislation. Students should understand that interpretation is not just a language exercise; it affects real rights, duties and outcomes.

  • Use key legal vocabulary accurately.
  • Apply the topic to a practical legal scenario using IRAC.
Detailed Self-Study Course Notes

Legal research is the skill of finding, selecting and using legal authorities. Students should begin with a clear research question. They should then identify whether the issue concerns statute, case law, procedure or academic debate. Reliable legal research requires checking whether a case is still good law, whether legislation has been amended, and whether the authority is relevant to the jurisdiction. Poor research often happens when students copy general online summaries without checking primary sources. Good research moves from broad understanding to precise authority. Students should keep a research log recording search terms, databases used, useful authorities and reasons for selecting them.

Week 7: Legal Writing

Learning Objectives
  • Understand the meaning of legal writing.
  • Use key legal vocabulary accurately.
  • Apply the topic to a practical legal scenario using IRAC.
Detailed Self-Study Course Notes

Legal writing should be clear, structured, accurate and evidence-based. Unlike creative writing, legal writing does not aim mainly to entertain. It aims to inform, advise, persuade or analyse. A strong legal paragraph usually states the legal point, supports it with authority, applies it to the facts and reaches a reasoned conclusion. Students should avoid vague statements such as 'this is unfair' unless they connect the point to a legal doctrine. Legal writing also requires careful tone. A client letter should be clear and practical, while an academic essay may be more analytical and critical. Precision is more important than complicated vocabulary.

Week 8: IRAC Method

Learning Objectives
  • Understand the meaning of irac method.
  • Use key legal vocabulary accurately.
  • Apply the topic to a practical legal scenario using IRAC.
Detailed Self-Study Course Notes

IRAC stands for Issue, Rule, Application and Conclusion. It is one of the most useful structures for legal problem questions. The Issue identifies the legal question. The Rule states the relevant law. The Application explains how the rule applies to the facts. The Conclusion gives the likely outcome. Many beginners spend too much time explaining the rule and too little time applying it. In law, the application is often the most valuable part because it shows reasoning. Students should use words such as 'because', 'therefore', 'however' and 'on these facts' to show the connection between law and facts.

Detailed Self-Study Course Notes

Legal ethics concerns the standards expected of legal professionals. Core ethical duties include honesty, confidentiality, independence, competence and avoiding conflicts of interest. Lawyers do not only serve clients; they also owe duties to the court and to the administration of justice. A lawyer must not mislead the court, misuse client money or reveal confidential information without proper authority. Ethical problems often involve tension between duties. For example, a lawyer may want to help a client strongly but must still act honestly. Students should understand that professional trust is essential to the legal system.

Detailed Self-Study Course Notes

Comparative legal study examines how different legal systems organise law and justice. Common law systems rely strongly on cases and precedent. Civil law systems rely more heavily on codes and legislation. Some systems are mixed, combining different traditions. Comparing systems helps students understand that legal rules are influenced by history, culture, politics and institutions. It also helps future lawyers work in international contexts. Students should not assume that one system is automatically superior. Instead, they should ask how each system solves problems such as certainty, fairness, access to justice and judicial power.

Week 11: Case Analysis

Learning Objectives
  • Understand the meaning of case analysis.
  • Use key legal vocabulary accurately.
  • Apply the topic to a practical legal scenario using IRAC.
Detailed Self-Study Course Notes

Case analysis is a central skill in law. A student should read a case to identify the material facts, legal issue, decision, reasoning and significance. The material facts are the facts that matter legally. The issue is the legal question the court had to answer. The decision is the outcome. The reasoning explains why the court reached that outcome. The significance shows how the case affects later law. Beginners often summarise too many background facts and miss the legal principle. A good case brief is short, accurate and focused on the rule that can be used in future problems.

Week 3: Mens Rea

Learning Objectives
  • Understand the meaning of mens rea.
  • Use key legal vocabulary accurately.
  • Apply the topic to a practical legal scenario using IRAC.
Detailed Self-Study Course Notes

Mens rea means the mental element of a crime. It may include intention, recklessness, knowledge or dishonesty, depending on the offence. Mens rea is important because criminal law usually distinguishes accidental harm from blameworthy conduct. Students should not assume every offence requires intention. Some offences require recklessness, and some are strict liability. Analysing mens rea requires close reading of the offence definition.

Week 4: Causation

Learning Objectives
  • Understand the meaning of causation.
  • Use key legal vocabulary accurately.
  • Apply the topic to a practical legal scenario using IRAC.
Detailed Self-Study Course Notes

Causation links the defendant’s conduct to a prohibited result. Factual causation asks whether the result would have happened but for the defendant’s conduct. Legal causation asks whether the defendant’s conduct was sufficiently connected to the result and whether any intervening act broke the chain. Causation is especially important in result crimes such as homicide or offences involving injury. Students should analyse causation step by step.
